In the fall, as the axis on which Earth spins leans away from the sun, the Northern Hemisphere sees less and less daylight.

Floridians, as they stop preparing for hurricanes and start preparing for school, see the sun rise later and later. By early November, the sun sleeps in as late as a quarter to 8.

Then, suddenly, at 2 a.m. on a Sunday, time shifts. When Daylight Saving Time ends (for most of the country), Floridians set their clock back an hour. This means sunrise starts much earlier again -- as early as it does in May or July.

The jump affects the evening too, when skies get dark an hour earlier -- often before those of us on the day shift can get home from work.

But all that could change. Florida wants to eliminate the fall-back, keeping year-round Daylight Saving Time. If the federal government says yes, what will that look like?
